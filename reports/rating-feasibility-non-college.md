# Rating System Feasibility — College vs. Non-College Markets
## A Comparative Customer Research & Marketing Psychology Report

*Last updated: 2026-04-11*
*Research mode: Behavioral synthesis + market segmentation + marketing psychology frameworks*
*Scope: Comparative analysis of rating/archetype/leaderboard adoption across two distinct target markets*

---

## Executive Summary

The honest answer to the question this report investigates is: **the rating, archetype, and leaderboard system is a fundamentally college-first product feature set.**

This is not a minor caveat — it is a core strategic insight that should shape how Ball Up Top communicates its value proposition to each market, sequences its expansion, and understands which features drive adoption where.

For the **college player** at a campus court: the full product loop (court discovery → check-in → rating → archetype → leaderboard) functions as a coherent, reinforcing system because the social preconditions for it already exist. There is a community. There is hierarchy. There is social capital at stake.

For the **casual gym player** at a random 24 Fitness in Orange County: the app will almost certainly be used as a court activity checker — *"is the court busy right now?"* — and nothing more. The rating, archetype, and leaderboard features will be noticed, possibly explored once, and then ignored. Not because the feature is bad. Because the environment doesn't generate the social conditions those features require to be meaningful.

This has significant product, marketing, and sequencing implications that this report outlines in full.

## Part 1: The Two Target Markets — Structural Differences

Before examining feature adoption, understand what is structurally different about these two environments. These differences are not incidental — they determine which psychological levers the product can and cannot pull.

---

### Market A: The College Court Player (Purdue/Big Ten Beachhead)

**Environment characteristics:**

- **Persistent community.** The same players show up to the same courts across an entire semester (4–5 months) and often across multiple years. You see the same faces weekly. Relationships — and reputations — accumulate.
- **Social hierarchy that matters.** "Who's the best player at the CoRec?" is a question that is genuinely asked and answered within campus pickup communities. The top player is known by name. Being ranked high means something in the social fabric of campus life.
- **Annual fresh cohort.** Every fall, a wave of incoming players — freshmen, transfers — needs to prove themselves and break into an existing scene. This creates perpetual status competition and perpetual new user demand.
- **Identity formation phase.** College-age players (18–22) are actively constructing their social identity. "I'm a ball player" is a meaningful part of who they are on campus, not a hobby they squeeze in between life.
- **Network density.** Campus social networks are tight. If one player on your floor uses Ball Up Top and you see their archetype, you want one too. Word spreads fast within a contained community.
- **Social enforcement.** If your friend or rival is checking in, there's implicit social pressure to do the same. Ambassador seeding creates visible behavioral norms that new users imitate.

**What this means for the product:**

The college environment provides the product with everything the social features need to function:
1. Persistent social relationships (so ratings accumulate meaning over time)
2. Real social stakes (so the leaderboard matters to real people in your real life)
3. Identity investment (so the archetype "feels like me" and you want to share it)
4. Network effects within a contained geography (so the app reaches critical mass faster)
5. Recurring fresh demand (so growth is structural, not just organic)

---

### Market B: The Casual Gym Player (24 Fitness, Orange County)

**Environment characteristics:**

- **Transient community.** The players at a 24 Fitness on a Tuesday night are a loose, rotating cast. Some are regulars, most are not. Memberships change. Work schedules change. The same 10 people are rarely at the court at the same time twice.
- **Anonymous social context.** Most gym players do not know each other's names. They may nod at a familiar face. They are strangers playing a shared activity, not members of a community.
- **No meaningful social hierarchy.** "Best player at the Crown Valley 24 Fitness" is not a title anyone covets or even conceptualizes. There is no audience for that status. It changes nothing in the real social lives of the people playing.
- **Settled identity.** The 26–35 adult gym player has a fully formed identity. Basketball is something they do, not something they are. "I'm a slasher archetype" is interesting for a moment — the way a personality quiz result is interesting — but it doesn't attach to a meaningful social context that amplifies its value.
- **Low network density.** Gym players are not in the same social graph. If one person at the court uses Ball Up Top, there's no channel through which that knowledge travels to others. No GroupMes, no dormitory floors, no intramural teams.
- **No fresh cohort injection.** Unlike a campus, a gym does not receive a wave of new members every fall who are seeking community and trying to establish themselves. Player turnover is random and low-signal.

**What this means for the product:**

The gym environment provides the product with almost none of the preconditions the social features require:
- No persistent relationships → ratings don't accumulate social meaning
- No social stakes → the leaderboard is a scoreboard no one is watching
- No identity investment → the archetype is a curiosity, not a statement
- No network density → word-of-mouth doesn't travel
- No fresh cohort → no structural growth driver

**What the environment DOES provide:**

- Real-time court activity curiosity ("how busy is it right now?")
- Basic court discovery need for new gym members or people who move
- Check-in curiosity (players may check in out of novelty or habit if prompted)
- Casual competition tracking for regulars who genuinely do know each other

## Part 2: Feature-by-Feature Adoption Forecast by Market

### 2.1 Court Discovery

**College:** High adoption. Players need to know where the run is happening across a campus with multiple courts. The app directly solves a genuine coordination problem.

**Gym (24 Fitness OC):** Medium adoption. The court discovery problem is less acute — there's one court, you know where it is. What matters here is occupancy, not location. Players want to know "is the court busy?" before driving to the gym. This is a real, if narrower, use case. A player at home on a Tuesday night who's debating whether to go to the gym would find real value in seeing "8 players currently checked in" before committing.

**Adoption verdict:** Both markets adopt court discovery, but for different reasons. College: coordination problem. Gym: occupancy check. The gym use case is real but thinner — it's a convenience feature, not a social infrastructure feature.

---

### 2.2 Check-In

**College:** Medium-High adoption (with ambassador friction). The check-in habit is learnable in a college environment because social enforcement is possible — if your friend is checking in and tells you to do it, you do it. Ambassador seeding creates visible behavioral norms. The community gives players a reason to want to be "seen" on the app.

**Gym (24 Fitness OC):** Low adoption outside of curiosity tries. The gym player has no social incentive to check in. There's no audience for their presence. No one in their social circle is using the app or will see that they're there. Without social enforcement, check-in becomes an unprompted, voluntary behavior with no immediate reward — exactly the kind of habit that doesn't form.

The geofence notification ("8 players checked in at [Court]") is the strongest mechanism to drive gym check-ins. It converts passive awareness into active behavior at the moment of maximum relevance. But without community density at the court, the notification doesn't fire — and without check-ins, density never builds.

**The gym check-in paradox:** Check-ins are only useful if other people are checking in. Other people only check in if there's social incentive. Social incentive requires community. Community requires persistent relationships. The gym environment breaks the chain before it starts.

**Adoption verdict:** College: behavioral habit achievable through seeding. Gym: check-in will happen once or twice, then stop unless critical mass is achieved at that specific court — which is far harder to engineer without the social infrastructure college provides.

---

### 2.3 Rating Encountered Players

This is the core question this report was commissioned to answer. Let's be specific.

**Will the 24 Fitness regular in Orange County actually rate opponents after a session?**

To rate, a player needs:
1. **To remember they can** — rating is a non-instinctive post-game behavior
2. **To feel it's worth their time** — what do they get from rating a stranger?
3. **To have a social relationship to the rated person** — rating someone feels meaningful if you'll see them again; it feels pointless if you won't
4. **To feel the system is fair** — if they think ratings are arbitrary, they won't participate

**The college player:**

- Has all four. They remember because their ambassador friend checks in and rates. It's worth their time because their own rating and archetype update. They have ongoing relationships with the people they're rating — they guard the same guy every Tuesday. They trust the system if a respected player on campus vouches for it.
- Rating completion rate projection: **45–65%** of completed sessions

**The gym player:**

- Has almost none of them. The rating screen appears after checkout — they may notice it once. Rating a stranger whose name they don't know feels abstract and pointless. Their own rating update isn't meaningful because no one in their real social world sees it or cares. They don't know if they'll ever see these people again.
- The specific psychology: Rating a stranger feels like reviewing a restaurant you'll never go to again. It requires effortful evaluation with no personal payoff.
- Exception: If they had a particularly great or particularly bad game — someone embarrassed them or they dominated — the emotional charge creates motivation. But this is the minority of sessions.
- Rating completion rate projection: **10–20%** of completed sessions, declining over time

**The ego problem:**

Pickup basketball has a specific social dynamic that makes rating strangers even harder in a low-community context: ego protection. Rating someone you played against well requires admitting they were good. In a college environment, where you'll see that person again and where your fair rating builds your own reputation as a credible rater, this is worth it. At the gym, where the social contract doesn't extend beyond one run, the ego calculus goes the other way — why give a stranger credit?

This is the "rating rivals" problem from the original customer research, amplified in the gym context where there's no community norm of fair play and credit-giving to offset ego.

---

### 2.4 Archetype Identity

**College:** High resonance. The archetype sits at the intersection of basketball identity and social sharing — both of which are heightened in a college environment. "I'm a 79 overall Slasher" is shareable, debatable, and sticky in a community where people know each other and basketball matters to their social identity. The "what's your archetype?" virality loop works because there's a community to be viral within.

**Gym:** Low resonance, high novelty. The gym player will be interested in their archetype when they first get one — the same way they'd be interested in a Myers-Briggs result or a Spotify Wrapped. But archetype identity requires a social mirror to reflect it back. If no one in your social world plays Ball Up Top or knows what a "Post Scorer" is, the archetype has nowhere to go. It's a fact about yourself with no audience.

The consumer psychology framework here is **social identity theory**: identity constructs are valuable proportional to the social groups that recognize and validate them. An archetype in a community of fellow hoopers is a social identity marker. An archetype known only to yourself is just a label.

**Adoption verdict:** Archetype exploration happens in both markets (novelty drives one-time engagement). Archetype identity — the sticky, sharing, "this is who I am" attachment — happens primarily in college. The gym player closes the archetype screen and doesn't think about it again.

---

### 2.5 Leaderboard & Player Database

**College:** High engagement potential. "Who's ranked #1 at the CoRec?" is a question players in a campus community genuinely ask and care about. The leaderboard surfaces status competition that already exists informally. It gives players a scoreboard for a competition they're already playing. Court-level leaderboards become a bragging right and a curiosity driver — checking the leaderboard is the digital equivalent of checking who showed up to the court.

**Gym:** Near zero engagement. A leaderboard of strangers at a gym is not interesting. There's no prior relationship to any of these people. No reason to care who ranks #1 at the Crown Valley 24 Fitness. The leaderboard lacks the social density to be a status competition — it's just a list.

The one exception: the small subset of gym regulars who do know each other by name and play together frequently. For this group — maybe 8–12 people at a given gym — a leaderboard could function like a small-community scoreboard. But this subset is hard to identify, harder to activate, and too small to justify as a primary market.

**Adoption verdict:** Leaderboard is a college feature. Full stop. It requires a pre-existing community that has social stakes in the rankings. The gym environment doesn't provide this.

## Part 3: The Behavioral Psychology Breakdown — Why College Works and Gyms Don't

### 3.1 The JTBD Mismatch

**Jobs-to-be-done analysis reveals the gap:**

| Job | College Player | Gym Player |
|-----|---------------|------------|
| Primary JTBD (utility) | Find where the run is happening | Know if the court is busy before I go |
| Primary JTBD (social) | Build a reputation that travels with me in my community | (No social JTBD — community is absent) |
| Secondary JTBD | Signal skill to new players; compete with known rivals | Track personal improvement loosely |
| Tertiary JTBD | Find new courts when visiting another campus | Find gyms with open courts when traveling |

The critical observation: the gym player's primary JTBD is entirely satisfiable by court discovery + check-in. The rating, archetype, and leaderboard features address a social JTBD that the gym player doesn't have.

This is not a marketing problem. It is a product-market fit problem at the feature level. You cannot market a social identity feature to someone who has no social community in which that identity has value.

---

### 3.2 The Five Preconditions for Social Feature Adoption

Marketing psychology research on social platforms and community-dependent features identifies five preconditions for adoption. Against each, compare the college vs. gym environment:

| Precondition | College Court | 24 Fitness Gym | Notes |
|-------------|--------------|----------------|-------|
| 1. Persistent social relationships | ✅ Strong | ❌ Absent | Rating means nothing without ongoing relationships |
| 2. Social stakes (status matters) | ✅ Strong | ❌ Absent | Leaderboard position must affect real social outcomes |
| 3. Identity investment in domain | ✅ High | ⚠️ Low-Medium | Basketball as identity vs. basketball as activity |
| 4. Network density for virality | ✅ High | ❌ Low | Archetypes spread in contained, connected communities |
| 5. Reciprocity norms | ✅ Present | ❌ Absent | Rating others requires expecting to be rated back fairly |

**Score: College 5/5. Gym 0–1/5.**

This table is the summary of why the social features are college-first.

---

### 3.3 The Psychology of Voluntary Post-Game Behavior

Rating a player after a game is a voluntarily, effortful, post-experience action. Behavioral psychology tells us these actions occur when the following conditions are met:

**Motivation triggers for post-game rating:**

1. **Reciprocal benefit** — "My rating gets better if I rate others" (Ball Up Top delivers this, but only if the player cares about their own rating)
2. **Social obligation** — "My community expects me to participate" (only present in college)
3. **Emotional charge** — "That game was amazing / terrible, I have to rate this person" (present in both, but rare)
4. **Habit** — "I always rate after a session" (achievable in college through ambassador seeding; nearly unachievable at a gym without social enforcement)

At a 24 Fitness:
- Reciprocal benefit exists — but requires caring about your rating, which requires having a social audience for your rating
- Social obligation is absent — no community, no expectation
- Emotional charge is occasional — happens for standout performances, not routine runs
- Habit cannot form without triggers — and triggers require community

**The Uber analogy (and why it doesn't apply at the gym):**

Uber's driver/rider rating system has near-universal completion because:
- Rating is prompted immediately in the moment (the app auto-navigates post-ride)
- Rating affects service quality you'll receive in the future (instrumental)
- Social norms around leaving reviews are already established
- It feels fair — you're rating someone who was paid to provide a service

Ball Up Top's rating system shares the first point (auto-navigate on checkout). But the others don't transfer to the gym context. Rating a stranger at a gym doesn't affect future service, there's no established norm for it, and rating someone who's trying to win a pickup game feels less fair and more intrusive than rating a ride.

---

### 3.4 The Social Hierarchy Problem

The original question frames this precisely: **is the rating/archetype/leaderboard system really feasible in a non-college environment where there is no existing element of social hierarchy and larger community?**

The answer is: **the social hierarchy isn't just helpful — it's load-bearing.**

Here's why. The product's social features are not creating social hierarchy from scratch. They are *formalizing existing informal hierarchy*. In a college pickup community, hierarchy already exists:
- There's a guy everyone knows is the best player
- There's a freshman who's trying to prove himself
- There's a regular who's been at this court for three years

Ball Up Top takes this existing social structure and gives it a digital layer. The ratings feel legitimate because they're *confirming* what players already know informally. The leaderboard feels meaningful because the rankings correspond to an existing social reality. The archetype feels accurate because it captures a player's actual role in a community that has watched them play.

At a 24 Fitness, there is no prior social structure to formalize. The app isn't confirming anything — it's asserting something about strangers who have no context for the assertion. Ratings feel arbitrary because there's no prior relationship to validate them. The leaderboard is a ranking of people who have no social relationship to each other. The archetype is a label with no community to recognize it.

**Marketing psychology term for this:** Ball Up Top's social features require **pre-existing social infrastructure** to activate. They are amplifiers of existing community, not generators of new community. This is a crucial and underappreciated distinction.

## Part 4: What the Non-College User Actually Will Use

Being precise here is important for product strategy. The non-college user isn't a lost cause — they're a different user with a different primary use case.

### The Actual Adoption Curve at a 24 Fitness

**Week 1 (Novelty):**
- Downloads app after seeing it mentioned or being told by a friend
- Checks out the court map, finds their local 24 Fitness listed
- Checks in at the court (novelty + curiosity)
- Gets their archetype after first few ratings (if any occur)
- Looks at the leaderboard once
- May or may not rate encountered players (mood-dependent)

**Weeks 2–4 (Reality check):**
- Checks the app before going to the gym: "how busy is the court?"
- Checks in some of the time, forgets other times
- Doesn't rate anyone (no motivation — strangers, no social stakes)
- Doesn't check the leaderboard (nothing interesting there)
- Archetype hasn't changed since week 1 (not enough ratings coming in)

**Month 2+ (Settled behavior):**
- Uses app to check court occupancy before going — this is the sticky behavior
- Everything else falls away
- May mention the app to a friend who plays at the same gym
- Will not share their archetype because no one in their social world cares

**What survives:** The court occupancy check. This is the real value for the gym demographic and it should be treated as such in marketing.

---

### The Exception: The Gym Regular Crew

There exists a segment of gym players who do have persistent social relationships: the crew of 8–12 regulars who play together 3–4x per week, know each other by name, have informal hierarchy among themselves, and genuinely compete against each other.

For this segment, Ball Up Top's social features *do* function — but only within that micro-community. The leaderboard is interesting because it's a scoreboard for a competition they care about. The archetype is shareable because the other regulars will see it and have opinions. Rating is reciprocal because they'll see the same people next week.

**The problem:** This segment is extremely hard to reach and activate as a cohort. Unlike a college campus — where the community already has communication channels (GroupMes, Instagram accounts, intramural programs) — the gym regular crew has no infrastructure through which to coordinate a collective app adoption. They need every member of their 8-person crew to download and use the app for the social features to function, and there's no mechanism to make this happen short of in-person recruiting.

**This is the "squad adoption" challenge:** Social features in small-community contexts require critical mass of *known individuals*, not just geographic critical mass. Getting 50 strangers to download the app at a 24 Fitness is worthless for the social features. Getting the same 8-person regular crew to all use it would unlock the full feature set — but coordinating that is manual, high-touch, and unscalable.

## Part 5: Implications for Go-to-Market Strategy

### 5.1 Market Sequencing — Validate Before Assuming Convergence

**College-first is correct — and not just for cold start reasons.**

The prior reports correctly identify Purdue as the beachhead. This report adds a deeper justification: the rating/archetype/leaderboard features only work in environments with pre-existing social infrastructure. College campuses are the highest-density environments for that infrastructure in the pickup basketball market. They should not just be the first market — they should be the *primary* market for the social feature set.

Non-college urban markets should be entered with a modified value proposition: **court discovery and occupancy as primary, social features as secondary.** Do not lead with "know your archetype and climb the leaderboard" at a 24 Fitness. Lead with "know if the court is live before you go." Let the social features develop organically if the regular crew at a given gym happens to all adopt the app.

---

### 5.2 Two Different Value Propositions, Same App

| Market | Lead Value Proposition | Secondary Proposition | Social Feature Positioning |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **College (Purdue/Big Ten)** | "Know where the run is happening" | "Build your rep — get your rating and archetype" | Front and center; it's the retention hook |
| **Non-college gym** | "Know if the court is busy before you drive" | "Check in, see who's there" | Background; let players discover it, don't lead with it |

This is not a failure of the product — it's precision marketing. The same app serves different jobs in different contexts. The error would be positioning the social features equally to both markets and watching gym users bounce when the features feel empty.

---

### 5.3 The Dangerous Assumption to Avoid

**Do not assume that once a non-college player sees the social features, they'll want to use them.** The standard growth assumption — "get users in the door with discovery, convert them to social features later" — may be wrong here.

This assumes the barrier to social feature adoption is awareness. The actual barrier is environmental: there's no community to adopt into. Showing the leaderboard to a gym player doesn't make them want to climb it — it makes them aware of a feature that isn't relevant to their life. The conversion to social features requires the environment to change (i.e., enough regulars at their gym to all adopt simultaneously), not just the player's awareness.

**The implication:** In non-college markets, design for a user who will remain a court-discovery user indefinitely. That's a valuable user — court occupancy data improves for every player whether they rate or not. Don't consider them a failure. Consider them a different product use case.

---

### 5.4 How to Market to Each Segment

**College market — Lead with identity and status:**

- "Build a rep that follows you"
- "What's your archetype?"
- "Climb the [School] CoRec leaderboard"
- Lead with the social features because the environment supports them
- Ambassador seeding creates the visible community that validates the product
- Instagram content: archetype reveals, leaderboard screenshots, milestone notifications
- The emotional hook: "finally, a way for people to know how good you actually are"

**Non-college gym market — Lead with utility:**

- "Know if the court is live before you drive"
- "See who's at the court before you get there"
- "Check in, ball out"
- Do not lead with archetypes, ratings, or leaderboards in marketing copy
- The emotional hook: "stop wasting trips to an empty court"
- If social features are shown, frame them as bonus discovery: "Plus, see who the regulars are and how they're rated" — plants the seed without overpromising

---

### 5.5 What to Do With the Gym Regular Crew Segment

This is the most viable path to social feature adoption in non-college markets. If Ball Up Top identifies and activates a specific gym's regular crew cohort, the full feature set unlocks. The approach:

1. **Identify court-level "connectors"** — at every gym, there's one person who knows everyone who plays there. This is the gym equivalent of a campus ambassador.
2. **Recruit the connector first**, then leverage their relationships to reach the rest of the crew.
3. **Set a lower critical mass bar** — a gym regular crew of 8 people is sufficient for the social features to function. This is achievable if you recruit the right connector.
4. **Accept that this is manual and unscalable at the current stage** — don't try to systematize this before the college model is proven. College first. Gym regular crew second. Mass-market gym third.

## Part 6: The Honest Risk Assessment for Social Features in Non-College Markets

### If Ball Up Top Tries to Grow Gym Markets Before Proving the College Model:

- Court discovery will generate downloads but low activation beyond first check-in
- Rating completion will be 10–20% at best
- Archetype features will feel hollow (not enough data, no community to share with)
- Leaderboard will be a list of strangers
- Churn will be high — the app delivered court occupancy, which is useful but not sticky enough alone to retain
- The brand risk: being known as a "court finder" rather than a "player reputation platform" forecloses the more valuable positioning

### If Ball Up Top Stays College-First and Enters Gyms Only After the College Model is Proven:

- The brand arrives in non-college markets with proof: "This app has 10,000 active users across 8 college campuses — now it's here"
- The social proof creates credibility that makes the social features more legible even to gym users
- Individual gym regulars who played college ball (the highest-propensity adopters in gym markets) will recognize the app and self-select in
- Court discovery utility is sufficient to build market presence in gym markets while the social features develop more slowly

## Part 8: Final Verdict

**Will regulars at a 24 Fitness in Orange County rate opponents after a session?**

**No — not at scale, not habitually, not with the same completeness or enthusiasm as college players.** They will check the occupancy before going. They will check in once or twice. They will look at their archetype when they first get one. They will ignore the leaderboard after the first look. They will not rate strangers consistently.

**Is the rating/archetype/leaderboard system feasible in a non-college environment?**

**Feasible in very specific sub-contexts (established gym regular crews) but not feasible as a mass-market feature for casual gym players.** The social features require social infrastructure that most non-college environments don't have. Without that infrastructure, the features exist but don't activate.

**What does this mean strategically?**

1. The college beachhead is not just about cold start — it's about launching the full product in the environment where the full product works.
2. Non-college markets are real and valuable, but they're primarily court-discovery markets, not social-feature markets, at this stage.
3. Marketing to each segment must use different value propositions. College: identity and reputation. Gym: utility and occupancy.
4. The path to social feature adoption in non-college markets runs through the "gym regular crew" segment, not the casual player — and it requires manual connector-based activation, not mass-market marketing.
5. The archetype and leaderboard features are the crown jewels of the product — protect their meaning by ensuring they first prove out in environments where they work, rather than diluting them by launching them in contexts where they'll feel hollow.

The product is not wrong. The social features are not overly ambitious. They are correctly designed for a specific type of environment. The strategic discipline required is deploying them in that environment first, proving them there, and then letting the proof do the marketing work when the product enters markets where the features need to earn their relevance.
